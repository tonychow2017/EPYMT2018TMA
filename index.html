Terminology

An equation is a statement of an equality containing one or more variables.
A solution to a given equation is a value or a tuple of value satisfying the equality. (Wikipedia: Equation)

Example:

  • The equation x 2=1 has a solution x=1.

Did you notice something special about the article I used in the above example? I said that the equation has a solution instead of the solution, because x=1 is not the only solution. x=-1 is also a solution.

We can also say that x=1 solves the equation.

Note, however, the verb solve is somewhat ambiguous. The reason is that while we can say something solves an equation, while you are asked to solve an equation (or a system of equations), you are expected to find out ALL solutions to the equation, at least all solutions in ℂ (that means, all complex solutions, if you don't know what ℂ is).

Therefore, when you are asked to solve the above equation, answering x=1 is not enough. You also need to include another solution: x=-1.

Challenge?

One thing that you may want to immediate challenge me is that, have we found out all solutions? Can there be any other solutions other than -1 and 1?

Fortunately, the answer is yes. However, because we are still in the prerequisite section, I am not going to explain this for the time being. Don't get worried, though. We will explain it later.

Quadratic equation:

A quadratic equation is an equation of the form

ax 2 + bx +c = 0

, where a, b and c are constants with a≠0, and x is the unknown (that we are going to find).

To solve the quadratic equation (we can also say, to find the roots of the polynomial ax 2 + b x + c), we have several methods.

Methods

  • Factorization:

    Factorize the LHS of the equation into the form

    (px+q)(rx+s)

    so that we have

    (px+q)(rx+s)=0

    . Now, since the product of two things is zero, at least one of them is zero (Think: Why?). So we have

    (px+q)=0 or (rx+s)=0

    . So we have

    x=-q p or -s r

    . Remember that there are two solutions.

  • Completing the square:

    We rewrite the LHS of the equation into the form

    a(x-h) 2+k

    so that we have

    a(x-h) 2+k=0

    . Now,

    (x-h) 2=-k a
    x-h=±-k a
    x=h±-k a

    . While it does involve a lot of algebraic manipulations in order to find out h and k, notice two points:

    1. h and k are useful. Since the square term is always non-negative, the polynomial attains its minimum (or maximum, depending on the sign of a) when x=h, and since the square term vanishes the value of the polynomial becomes k.
    2. How about if we are going to derive a general formula for h and k? Let's do it now.

  • Quadratic formula:

    Now do the algebraic operations:

    ax 2+bx+c
    =a(x 2+b ax)+c
    =a(x 2 - 2(-b 2a)x)+c
    =a(x 2-2x(-b 2a)+(-b 2a) 2-(-b 2a) 2)+c
    =a(x 2-2x(-b 2a)+(-b 2a) 2)-a(-b 2a) 2+c
    =a(x-(-b 2a)) 2 + (c-a(-b 2a) 2)

    It is easy to see that

    h=-b 2a  and  k=c-ah 2

    , and hence,

    x
    =-b 2a±h 2-c a
    =-b 2 a±b 2 4a 2-4ac 4a 2
    =-b±b 2-4ac 2a

    . This is known as the Quadratic Formula.

      Quadratic formula: -b±b 2-4ac 2a  

Examples

  • Solve the equation x 2-9x+20=0.
    Method 1:

    x 2-9x+20
    =(x-4)(x-5)

    Therefore,

    x-4=0 or x-5=0
    x=4 or x=5

    Method 2:

    x 2+9x+20
    =x 2+2x(9 2)+20
    =x 2+2x(9 2)+(9 2) 2-(9 2) 2+20
    =(x+9 2) 2-1 4

    Therefore, from the original equation,

    (x+9 2) 2=1 4
    x+9 2=±1 2
    x=5 or 4

    Method 3:

    Use the Quadratic Formula:
    a=1, b=-9, c=20;

    x
    =-9±(-9) 2-4(1)(20) 2(1)
    =-9±1 2
    =4 or 5

3. Intermediate Value Theorem

Explanation

Although the theorem is simple and intuitive, proving it formally is actually quite hard.
If, in the future, you are good enough to be admitted to CUHK, you may learn the proof (which we are going to skip) in MATH2050.
However, it would be interesting to investigate some other related results.

Continuity

If the function is not continuous, the result may not be true.
An example is the reciprocal function, say, f, defined by f(x)=1 x for all real x. It is negative when x is negative, and is positive when x is positive. However, it has no root on the interval [-M,M] for any M>0. The reason for which the Intermediate Value Theorem does not hold is that f has a discontinuity at x=0.

The Converse

The converse is simply not true. The constant function y=0 is an example. Indeed, one cannot find any a and b satisfying f(a)<0 and f(b)>0.

4. Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Explanation

The theorem guarantees that a polynomial equation with complex coefficnets must have at least one complex root. Again, since proving it is hard (take MATH2230 if you want), we will skip the proof.

Significance

A corollary is that, counted with multiplicity, a polynomial equation with degree n must have n solution(s).
This is because, when we found one solution (Note: This must succeed, guaranteed by the theorem), we can do factorization and reduce the degree by 1. So altogether we can factorize the polynomial into a product of n linear polynomial(s).
But wait...what is the meaning of multiplicity? Simply said, when a quadratic polynomial has a repeated root, the root is said to be of multiplicity 2. When it is a distinct root the multiplicity is 1.
