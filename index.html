Terminology

An equation is a statement of an equality containing one or more variables.
A solution to a given equation is a value or a tuple of value satisfying the equality. (Wikipedia: Equation)

Example:

  • The equation x 2=1 has a solution x=1.

Did you notice something special about the article I used in the above example? I said that the equation has a solution instead of the solution, because x=1 is not the only solution. x=-1 is also a solution.

We can also say that x=1 solves the equation.

Note, however, the verb solve is somewhat ambiguous. The reason is that while we can say something solves an equation, while you are asked to solve an equation (or a system of equations), you are expected to find out ALL solutions to the equation, at least all solutions in ℂ (that means, all complex solutions, if you don't know what ℂ is).

Therefore, when you are asked to solve the above equation, answering x=1 is not enough. You also need to include another solution: x=-1.

Challenge?

One thing that you may want to immediate challenge me is that, have we found out all solutions? Can there be any other solutions other than -1 and 1?

Fortunately, the answer is yes. However, because we are still in the prerequisite section, I am not going to explain this for the time being. Don't get worried, though. We will explain it later.

Quadratic equation:

A quadratic equation is an equation of the form

ax 2 + bx +c = 0

, where a, b and c are constants with a≠0, and x is the unknown (that we are going to find).

To solve the quadratic equation (we can also say, to find the roots of the polynomial ax 2 + b x + c), we have several methods.

Methods

  • Factorization:

    Factorize the LHS of the equation into the form

    (px+q)(rx+s)

    so that we have

    (px+q)(rx+s)=0

    . Now, since the product of two things is zero, at least one of them is zero (Think: Why?). So we have

    (px+q)=0 or (rx+s)=0

    . So we have

    x=-q p or -s r

    . Remember that there are two solutions.

  • Completing the square:

    We rewrite the LHS of the equation into the form

    a(x-h) 2+k

    so that we have

    a(x-h) 2+k=0

    . Now,

    (x-h) 2=-k a
    x-h=±-k a
    x=h±-k a

    . While it does involve a lot of algebraic manipulations in order to find out h and k, notice two points:

    1. h and k are useful. Since the square term is always non-negative, the polynomial attains its minimum (or maximum, depending on the sign of a) when x=h, and since the square term vanishes the value of the polynomial becomes k.
    2. How about if we are going to derive a general formula for h and k? Let's do it now.

  • Quadratic formula:

    Now do the algebraic operations:

    ax 2+bx+c
    =a(x 2+b ax)+c
    =a(x 2 - 2(-b 2a)x)+c
    =a(x 2-2x(-b 2a)+(-b 2a) 2-(-b 2a) 2)+c
    =a(x 2-2x(-b 2a)+(-b 2a) 2)-a(-b 2a) 2+c
    =a(x-(-b 2a)) 2 + (c-a(-b 2a) 2)

    It is easy to see that

    h=-b 2a  and  k=c-ah 2

    , and hence,

    x
    =-b 2a±h 2-c a
    =-b 2 a±b 2 4a 2-4ac 4a 2
    =-b±b 2-4ac 2a

    . This is known as the Quadratic Formula.

      Quadratic formula: x=-b±b 2-4ac 2a  

Examples

  • Solve the equation x 2-9x+20=0.
    Method 1:

    x 2-9x+20
    =(x-4)(x-5)

    Therefore,

    x-4=0 or x-5=0
    x=4 or x=5

    Method 2:

    x 2+9x+20
    =x 2+2x(9 2)+20
    =x 2+2x(9 2)+(9 2) 2-(9 2) 2+20
    =(x+9 2) 2-1 4

    Therefore, from the original equation,

    (x+9 2) 2=1 4
    x+9 2=±1 2
    x=5 or 4

    Method 3:

    Use the Quadratic Formula:
    a=1, b=-9, c=20;

    x
    =-9±(-9) 2-4(1)(20) 2(1)
    =-9±1 2
    =4 or 5

2. Solving Cubic and Quartic Equations

Explanation

In this part, I will illustrate the algebraic methods of solving cubic equations with several examples. I expect that you have already read the lecture notes, so it would be redundant to mention them again.

Examples

Sometimes, you can find out one of the roots very quickly. In this case, it is more convenient to carry out long division and reduce the problem to a quadratic one.

  • Solve the equation x 3-10x 2+29x-20=0.

    Indeed, it is easy(?) to observe that x=1 is a solution. Carry out long division (which I assume you know how to do it; ask me if you don't) and we get

    x 3-10x 2-29x+20
    =(x-1)(x 2-9x+20)

    Therefore, x=1 or 4 or 5. (If you don't know where 4 and 5 come, read the last section.)

    This is indeed straightforward. However, it relies on the fact that you are capable of guessing one of the roots. While normally you can make a guess of x=1, no one expects you to correctly guess the solution if it is something like x=41 152.

    Now we try to solve the equation without guessing any solution in advance.
    Let x=y-(-10) 3=y+10 3. Then

    LHS
    =(y+10 3) 3-10(y+10 3) 2+29(y+10 3)-20
    =y 3+3(10 3)y 2+3(10 3) 2y+(10 3) 3-10(y 2+20 3y+(10 3) 2)+29y+290 3-20
    =y 3+(100 3-200 3+29)y+(1000 27-1000 9+290 3-20)
    =y 3-13 3y+70 27

    Now, let y=α+β.

    (α+β) 3=3αβ(α+β)+(α 3+β 3).

    Now we put

    -3αβ=-13 3 and -(α 3+β 3)=70 27
    (αβ) 3=(13 9) 3 and α 3+β 3=-70 27.

    Now let α'=α 3 and β'=β 3.

    α'(-70 27-α')=(13 9) 3
    α' 2+70 27α'+(13 9) 3=0.

    Solving the equation (by quadratic formula) and noticing the symmetry (between α' and β'), we get

    α'=-35 27+2i 3 and β'=-35 27-2i 3.

    Taking square root: since

    (-35 27)2+(2 3) 2=1313 27,acos(-35 27 1313 27)≈138.30634126°,13 13 27 3=(13) 3 27 3=13 3

    , we have

    (α,β)
    =(13 3(cos(138.30634126+360k)° 3+isin(138.30634126+360k)° 3),13 3(cos-(138.30634126+360k)° 3+isin-(138.30634126+360k)° 3))

    , where k=0,1,2.
    So,

    y
    =α+β
    =13 3(2cos(138.30634126+360k)° 3),k=0,1,2
    =1.6667,-2.3333 or 0.6667.

    Adding 10 3 to y, we conclude that x=5,1 or 4.

  • Solve the equation x 3-6x 2+11x 2-6=0.

    Notice that after we do the substitution x=y+2, LHS =y 3-y, which has constant term 0. You can solve this equation directly (the substitution y=α+β being unnecessary and inefficient).
    The solutions are x=1 or 2 or 3.

  • Solve the equation x 3-59 8x 2+499 64x-441 512=0.

    Note: I am skipping some of the steps here (key steps are retained). You should be able to reproduce them.
    Let x=y--59 8 3(1)=y+59 24. Then

    LHS
    =(y+59 24) 3-59 8(y+59 24) 2+499 64(y+59 24)-441 512
    =y 3-31 3y-308 27.

    Let y=α+β. Then we put

    -3αβ=-31 3 and -(α 3+β 3)=-308 27.

    Let α'=α 3 and β'=β 3. Then

    α'(308 27-α')=(31 9) 3
    α' 2-308 27α'+(31 9) 3=0
    α'=154 27+5i 3 and β'=154 27-5i 3

    Since

    (154 27) 2+(5 3) 2=3131 27=(31 3) 3,acos(154 27 3131 27)=26.84483°

    , we have

    α+β=31 3(2cos((26.84483+360k)° 3)),k=0,1,2
    y=11 3,-7 3 or -4 3
    x=49 8,1 8 or 9 8.

  • Solve the equation x 4+x 3-7x 2-x+6=0.
    Notice that this is a quartic equation (the degree is 4).
    Let x=y-1 4(1)=y-1 4. Then

    LHS
    =(y-1 4) 4+(y-1 4) 3-7(y-1 4) 2-(y-1 4)+6
    =(y 4+4y 3(-1 4)+6y 2(-1 4) 2+4y(-1 4) 3+(-1 4) 4)+(y 3+3y 2(-1 4)+3y(-1 4) 2+(-1 4) 3)-7(y 2-1 2y+(-1 4) 2)-y+1 4+6
    =y 4+(3 8-3 4-7)y 2+(-1 16+3 16+7 2-1)y+(1 256-1 64-7 16+1 4+6)
    =y 4-59 8y 2+21 8y+1485 256

    Now, by completing the square,

    y 4-59 8y 2=-21 8y-1485 256
    y 4-59 8y 2+(59 16) 2=-21 8y-1485 256+(59 16) 2
    (y 2-59 16) 2+2(y 2-59 16)m+m 2=-21 8y+499 64+2(y 2-59 16)m+m 2

    . In order to make the RHS a complete square, we want the discriminant to be 0.

    RHS
    =2my 2-21 8y+(499 64-59 8m+m 2)

    So we want

    (-21 8) 2-4(2m)(499 64-59 8m+m 2)=0
    -8m 3+59m 2-499 8m+441 64=0
    m 3-59 8m 2+449 64-441 512=0

    . From the last example we know that one of the solutions is

    m=1 8

    . So

    (y 2-59 16+1 8) 2=1 4y 2-21 8y+441 64
    (y 2-57 16) 2=(1 2y-21 8) 2
    y 2-57 16=±(1 2y-21 8)
    y 2-57 16=1 2y-21 8 or y 2-57 16=-1 2y+21 8

3. Intermediate Value Theorem

Explanation

Although the theorem is simple and intuitive, proving it formally is actually quite hard.
If, in the future, you are good enough to be admitted to CUHK, you may learn the proof (which we are going to skip) in MATH2050.
However, it would be interesting to investigate some other related results.

Continuity

If the function is not continuous, the result may not be true.
An example is the reciprocal function, say, f, defined by f(x)=1 x for all real x. It is negative when x is negative, and is positive when x is positive. However, it has no root on the interval [-M,M] for any M>0. The reason for which the Intermediate Value Theorem does not hold is that f has a discontinuity at x=0.

Significance

We can make numerical guesses of solutions of arbitrary equations (without loss of generality, assuming that RHS equals 0) as long as RHS takes both positive and negative values.

Example

  • Let f be a function defined by f(x)=2x 3-3x 2-x+1 for all real x.
    Calculate f(0.45) and f(0.55). Hence solve the equation f(x)=0.

    f(0.45)=0.12475 and f(0.55)=-0.12475.
    Therefore, a good guess of a solution is x=1 2.
    It can be verified that f(0.5)=0.
    By long division, f(x)=(2x-1)(x 2-x-1).
    So the solutions are x=1 2,1+5 2 or 1-5 2.



4. Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Explanation

The theorem guarantees that a polynomial equation with complex coefficnets must have at least one complex root. Again, since proving it is hard (take MATH2230 if you want), we will skip the proof.

Significance

A corollary is that, counted with multiplicity, a non-zero polynomial equation with degree n must have n solution(s).
This is because, when we found one solution (Note: This must succeed, guaranteed by the theorem), we can do factorization and reduce the degree by 1. So altogether we can factorize the polynomial into a product of n linear polynomial(s).
But wait...what is the meaning of multiplicity? Simply said, when a quadratic polynomial has a repeated root, the root is said to be of multiplicity 2. When it is a distinct root the multiplicity is 1.
But in order to define the notion of multiplicity, we need to define the meaning of a factor, or touch some analysis (Remember? This is an algebra class). I will skip it for now, but feel free to discuss it with me later.

Remember the challenge I mentioned when I talked about quadratic equations? You should now understand that a quadratic equation always has two solutions (with multiplicity).

Examples

  • Suppose a degree n polynomial has n+1 roots. Then it must be identical to 0.
  • Suppose there exists a polynomial of degree n whose graph passes n+1 points. Then such a polynomial must be unique.
    Reason: Suppose both f and g pass through those n+1 points. Then f-g is a polynomial of degree at most n which has n+1 roots. Therefore f-g must be identical to 0 The process of finding such polynomial is called interpolation.
